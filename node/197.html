Transcription

Posa nel sen della gran Madre, e siede,
E de Pastori humil l’alme devote
Gli porgono in tributo oro di fede.

4
Fig.a mezzana in tela di p.i 5 di larg.a, e 4 d’alt.a

Fa restar di stupor le genti immote
Del gran Vandich un Polifemo atroce,
Che con parte di un monte Acis percote

Dal Ciclopo crudel ch’altrui si noce
Fugge reggendo a suoi delfini il freno
Su la conchiglia Galatea veloce,

E scorgonsi avventarle i dardi al seno
Gli Amoretti volanti, e a nuoto intorno
I squamosi Triton nel mar sereno.

5
Fig.e al naturale sino al ginochio in tela di p.i 5½ di largh.a, e 4½ di alt.a

Di colorito egregio, e di contorno
Della Vergine in grembo Iddio Bambino
Il Palma figurò di gratie adorno,

Che dal S. Gioseppe a lui vicino
Lieto un pomo riceve all’hor che intent
Mostrano altri notar l’atto divino.

6
Fig.e piccole in tela di p.i 5 di largh.a, e di 4½ d’alt.a

Angelica nel suol co i rai dolenti,
Che s’affanna a Medor sanar la piaga
Del dittamo co i sughi in lei cadenti

Fu dal Lanfranco colorita, e gaga
Mostra la tela nel fatal successo,
Che mentre a lui da vita Amor lei piaga.

English

He rests on the breast of the great Mother and sits
While humble shepherds with devoted hearts
Make Him an offering of the gold of faith.

4.
Medium-size figure on canvas, 5 palms wide and 4 high

A horrific Polyphemus by the great Van Dyck
Makes the people stand stock still with awe
As the giant strikes Acis with a huge boulder.

Galatea, on a seashell,
Holding the reins of her dolphins,
Swiftly flees the cruel, violent Cyclops.

Fluttering putti shoot arrows into her breast
And scaly Tritons swim around her
In the serene sea.

5.
Life-size figures, three-quarter-length on canvas, 5½ palms wide and 4½ high

With singular colors and contours
Palma depicted the Virgin with the Christ Child
In her lap, adorned with graces.

From St. Joseph, who stands near him
The Babe happily accepts an apple
While onlookers intently observe the divine act.

Angelica on the ground with sorrowful eyes
Toils to heal Medoro,
Dropping the juice of dittany on his wound.

6.
Small figures on canvas, 5 palms wide and 4½ high

The exquisite canvas was painted by Lanfranco
And shows the fatal moment
When Angelica is wounded by Love as she revives Medoro.

This is a reference to Ariosto’s Orlando furioso, first published in its complete form in 1532. —Murtha Baca
These allusions to the capacity of images to affect the viewer visually and emotionally are a constant in the poem; the words “stupore” and “stupire” recur with some frequency. See also the description (and editorial notes) on part 1, no. 19 (folio 6 verso); and in the fourth stanza on folio 7 verso. For Pietro, in the tradition of ekphrasis, the intellectual approach to the artworks is subordinated to the emotional and visual one. —Nuria Rodríguez Ortega
In the 1680 inventory, Joseph is described as offering the Christ Child a bird (“ucelletto”) rather than an apple (“pomo”). This is one of numerous indications that Pietro was not directly observing the paintings when he wrote this poem but was probably describing them from memory. —Helen Glanville
The adjective “vago” is one of the critical terms that Pietro uses most frequently in the poem. Although used in different ways, since the fifteenth century the noun “vaghezza” had been used to refer to a delicate, pleasant, and sensually pleasing type of beauty, closely linked to the smooth, thick application of paints. See also the descriptions of part 1, no. 15 (folio 3 recto); part 1, nos. 37 and 38 (folio 5 recto); part 2, no. 6 (folio 8 recto); part 2, no. 16 (folio 9 verso); and part 2, no. 24 (folio 10 recto). Pietro uses the adjective “vago/vaga” to describe various pictorial elements, but he employs it most often in the description of the visual and emotional impact of landscapes.
—Nuria Rodríguez Ortega
An alternate translation would be “Superb both in color and design.”
—Helen Glanville
